# TaskVault — Brand Voice Guide

---

## 1. Overall Tone

TaskVault's support voice is: **Friendly, Professional, Efficient.**

We sound like a smart, helpful coworker — not a robot, not a corporate hotline, and not an overly casual friend. We're warm without being fluffy, knowledgeable without being condescending, and fast without being dismissive.

**Our voice pillars:**
- **Clear:** No jargon. No ambiguity. Say what you mean.
- **Empathetic:** Acknowledge the customer's experience before jumping to solutions.
- **Confident:** We know our product. We don't hedge unnecessarily.
- **Action-oriented:** Every response should move the customer closer to a solution.

---

## 2. Channel-Specific Tone Adjustments

### Email (Gmail)

**Tone:** Formal but warm. Structured and detailed.

- Always open with a greeting using the customer's first name.
- Use clear paragraph structure: empathy → solution → next steps.
- Provide step-by-step instructions when relevant.
- Close with a named sign-off.
- It's okay to be slightly longer — email customers expect thorough replies.

**Structure:**
```
Hi [Name],

[Empathy/Acknowledgment — 1 sentence]

[Solution — detailed, with steps if needed]

[Next steps or follow-up — 1-2 sentences]

Best regards,
TaskVault Support
```

### WhatsApp

**Tone:** Casual, concise, conversational.

- Keep messages under 300 characters when possible.
- Use simple, direct language. One idea per message.
- Okay to use 1-2 emojis per message (see Emoji Policy below).
- Don't write walls of text — break into multiple short messages if needed.
- Match the customer's energy and formality level.
- No formal sign-offs — just end naturally.

### Web Form

**Tone:** Semi-formal, helpful, balanced.

- Acknowledge that the customer took the time to fill out a form.
- Reply via email (responses to web form submissions go to the customer's email).
- Be structured but not as formal as pure email.
- Include relevant links to help docs when applicable.

## 3. Words and Phrases to USE

| Category | Approved Language |
|---|---|
| **Greetings** | "Hi [Name]!", "Hey [Name],", "Hello [Name]," |
| **Empathy** | "I understand how frustrating that can be.", "That makes total sense.", "I'm sorry you're running into this.", "Thanks for letting us know." |
| **Confidence** | "Here's how to fix that:", "This should solve it:", "Great news — there's a quick fix.", "Let me walk you through it." |
| **Helpfulness** | "Here's what I'd recommend:", "You can find this in Settings > ...", "Let me know if you need anything else." |
| **Closing** | "Hope that helps!", "Let me know if you have any other questions.", "Happy to help anytime!", "We're here if you need us." |
| **Transitions** | "Just to make sure I understand...", "To clarify:", "One more thing:" |
| **Positive** | "Absolutely!", "Great question!", "Of course!", "You're all set!" |

---

## 4. Words and Phrases to NEVER USE

| Forbidden | Why | Use Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Unfortunately" | Sounds negative and deflecting | "Here's what we can do:" |
| "As per my previous email" | Passive-aggressive | "Just to follow up on this:" |
| "That's not possible" | Closes the door | "That feature isn't available yet, but here's an alternative:" |
| "You need to..." | Commanding tone | "You can..." or "Here's how to..." |
| "Obviously" | Condescending | Remove entirely |
| "Actually" (correcting the customer) | Condescending | "Just to clarify:" |
| "Please be advised" | Overly formal/legal | Simply state the information |
| "We apologize for any inconvenience" | Generic and hollow | "I'm sorry about this — let me fix it for you." |
| "That's a known issue" | Dismissive | "We're aware of this and working on it. In the meantime, here's a workaround:" |
| "ASAP" | Vague urgency | Give a specific timeline |
| "No worries" (after a serious issue) | Minimizes the customer's concern | "I completely understand your concern." |
| "Per our policy" | Hides behind rules | Explain the reasoning |
| "It's in the docs" (without a link) | Lazy and dismissive | Provide the exact link and quote the relevant section |
| "FYI" | Too casual for support | "Just so you know:" or "For your reference:" |

## 5. Handling Angry Customers

### The Empathy-First Approach (E.A.R. Method)

1. **Empathize:** Acknowledge their frustration explicitly. Don't skip this step.
   - "I completely understand your frustration, and I'm sorry you're dealing with this."

2. **Acknowledge:** Restate their issue to show you heard them.
   - "It sounds like you've been unable to access your project data since yesterday."

3. **Resolve:** Provide the solution or clear next steps.
   - "Here's exactly what I'm going to do to fix this:"

### Rules for Angry Customers
- NEVER match their anger. Stay calm and professional.
- NEVER say "calm down" or "relax."
- NEVER blame the customer, even if they made an error.
- Acknowledge the emotion, then pivot to the solution.
- If they use profanity, don't address the language — just solve the problem.
- If they make threats (legal, public, etc.), escalate immediately per escalation rules.

### Example:

**Customer:** "This is absolutely RIDICULOUS. I've been locked out of my account for 2 days and nobody has helped me. Your support is a JOKE."

**Bad response:** "I apologize for any inconvenience. Unfortunately, account lockouts are a security measure. You need to wait 15 minutes and try again."

**Good response:** "I'm really sorry about this, Marcus. Being locked out for 2 days is completely unacceptable, and I understand your frustration. Let me look into your account right now and get you back in. Can you confirm the email address on your account?"

## 6. Handling Confused Customers

- Don't assume they know product terminology.
- Use simple, everyday language.
- Break down steps into numbered lists.
- Offer to walk them through it step-by-step.
- If their question is vague, ask one clarifying question — don't ask multiple questions at once.
- Repeat their question back in plain language to confirm understanding.

### Example:

**Customer (WhatsApp):** "it's not working"

**Bad response:** "Could you please provide more details about the issue you're experiencing? What feature are you trying to use? What error message do you see? What device and browser are you using?"

**Good response:** "Hey! Sorry about that. Can you tell me what you were trying to do when it stopped working? That way I can help you right away."

## 7. Opening and Closing Conversations

### Email

**Opening:**
- "Hi [Name], thanks for reaching out!"
- "Hi [Name], thanks for contacting TaskVault support."
- "Hello [Name], I'm happy to help with this."

**Closing:**
- "If you have any other questions, don't hesitate to reach out. Best regards, TaskVault Support"
- "Hope that clears things up! Let us know if you need anything else. Best, TaskVault Support"

### WhatsApp

**Opening:**
- "Hey [Name]! 👋 How can I help?"
- "Hi [Name]! What's going on?"
- "Hey! Thanks for reaching out."

**Closing:**
- "All good now? Let me know if anything else comes up!"
- "Glad I could help! Have a great day."
- "You're all set! Reach out anytime."

### Web Form

**Opening:**
- "Hi [Name], thanks for submitting your request."
- "Hi [Name], I received your message and I'm happy to help."

**Closing:**
- "I hope this resolves your issue. If not, just reply to this email and I'll follow up. Best regards, TaskVault Support"
- "Let me know if you have any follow-up questions. We're here to help! Best, TaskVault Support"

## 8. Example Good vs Bad Responses

### Scenario 1: Customer can't find the export feature

**Email — Bad:**
"The export feature is in Settings. Please check the documentation for more details."

**Email — Good:**
"Hi Sarah, great question! You can export your data by going to **Settings > Workspace > Data Export**. From there, choose what you'd like to export (tasks, projects, etc.) and your preferred format (CSV or JSON). Once you click **Generate Export**, you'll receive a download link via email within a few minutes. Let me know if you need help with anything else! Best regards, TaskVault Support"

### Scenario 2: Customer's integration isn't working

**WhatsApp — Bad:**
"I apologize for the inconvenience. Please navigate to Settings, then Integrations, then Slack. Check if the connection status shows Connected. If it shows Disconnected, please click the Reconnect button and authorize the application again through the OAuth popup. Also make sure the TaskVault app hasn't been removed from your Slack workspace."

**WhatsApp — Good:**
"Oh no! Let's fix that quick. Can you go to Settings > Integrations > Slack and check if it says Connected or Disconnected?"

*(Then after customer responds)*

"Ah, it's disconnected. Just hit Reconnect and approve the popup. Should be back in seconds! 🔧"

### Scenario 3: Customer is upset about being charged

**Web Form — Bad:**
"Unfortunately, as per our cancellation policy, charges incurred before the cancellation date are non-refundable. We apologize for any inconvenience."

**Web Form — Good:**
"Hi Alex, I understand how confusing unexpected charges can be. When you cancelled, the current billing cycle was already active, so that charge covers the period you had access. Going forward, you won't be charged again. If you'd like, I can look into whether you're eligible for a refund under our 14-day guarantee. Could you let me know when you originally signed up? Best regards, TaskVault Support"

## 9. Emoji Usage Policy

| Channel | Emoji Allowed? | Rules |
|---|---|---|
| **Email** | No | Never use emojis in email responses. |
| **WhatsApp** | Yes | Max 1-2 per message. Use sparingly to add warmth, not to replace words. |
| **Web Form** | No | Responses go via email — same rule as email. |

### Approved Emojis for WhatsApp
- 👋 (greeting)
- ✅ (confirmation / done)
- 🔧 (fixing something)
- 💡 (tip or idea)
- 🙌 (celebration / positive)
- ⏳ (waiting / processing)

### Forbidden Emojis (All Channels)
- 😂 🤣 (laughing — can seem mocking)
- 🙄 (dismissive)
- 💀 (inappropriate)
- ❤️ (too personal)
- Any emoji that could be misinterpreted in a professional context

## 10. Formatting Guidelines

### Email
- Use **bold** for UI elements (e.g., **Settings > Billing**).
- Use numbered lists for multi-step instructions.
- Keep paragraphs to 2-3 sentences.
- Use line breaks generously for readability.

### WhatsApp
- No markdown formatting (WhatsApp has its own: *bold*, _italic_).
- Keep messages short. If you need to explain multiple steps, send them as separate messages.
- Use `>` for quoting the customer's message if needed.

### Web Form (Email Reply)
- Same as email formatting rules.
- Reference the form submission: "Regarding your request about [subject]..."
